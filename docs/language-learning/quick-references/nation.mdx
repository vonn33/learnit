# Nation's Framework — Quick Reference

**Paul Nation · Victoria University of Wellington, 2014**
*What Do You Need to Know to Learn a Foreign Language?*

Nation's core insight: learning effectiveness depends on **conditions**, not method loyalty. Any method that reliably creates the right cognitive conditions will work.

---

## The 4 Core Principles

Every recommendation in the book flows from these four principles. They are not sequential steps — all four operate simultaneously.

1. **Work out your needs and learn what's most useful** — be clear about your goal; prioritise high-frequency vocabulary; test what you already know
2. **Balance across the four strands (equal time each)** — Meaning-focused input · Meaning-focused output · Language study · Fluency development
3. **Apply the conditions that help learning** — Spaced repetition, retrieval practice, varied meetings, deliberate attention
4. **Keep motivated and work hard** — Set weekly goals; track progress; do something every day

---

## The Four Strands

Spend roughly equal time (~25% each) on all four. Most learners over-invest in deliberate study (Strand 3) and neglect fluency (Strand 4) and output (Strand 2).

**Key vocabulary rules:**
- **Frequency first** — Top 100 words = ~50% of any text. The payoff per word is enormous in the high-frequency zone.
- **No interference sets** — Never study near-synonyms, opposites, or lexical sets (colours, days, months) together. Interference makes retention 50–100% harder.
- **Narrative sets are fine** — Words connected by a story or scene (frog, pond, hop, green) do not interfere.
- **Word knowledge is multidimensional** — Full knowledge = form + meaning + use. Flashcards alone build only the form-meaning link.

<Callout label="The Keyword Technique">
Find an L1 word that sounds like the target word → create a vivid image linking the L1 meaning to the L2 meaning. Produces ~25% better retention than other deliberate strategies.
</Callout>

## Strand 1 — Meaning-Focused Input

Rule of thumb: no more than 2 unknown words per 100 running words. Material above this threshold is too hard to learn from effectively.

**Reading:** Graded readers (Oxford Bookworms, Cambridge Readers, Penguin Readers) → Mid-Frequency Readers at 4k, 6k, 8k levels. Re-reading is underrated — it guarantees repetition and builds fluency. Narrow reading (staying in one topic area) reduces vocabulary load efficiently.

**Listening:** Listen to the target language even without understanding early on — it builds phonological familiarity. Get a film + its script: read the script first, watch with subtitles, watch again weeks later.

<Callout label="Reading While Listening">
Listening to a recording while silently reading the same text produces higher vocabulary scores and comprehension than listening alone.
</Callout>

---

## Strand 2 — Meaning-Focused Output

Speaking and writing where communication is the goal — not accuracy. Producing language forces you to notice gaps in your knowledge.

**Speaking:** Start with memorised survival phrases. Practice role plays of real-world scenarios. Prepared talks: write short talks on likely topics, memorise them, practise until fluent. Arrange regular conversation with a native speaker.

**Writing:** Accuracy writing (draft carefully, get corrected) + fluency writing (10-minute writing — write continuously, do not stop to correct).

---

## Strand 3 — Language-Focused Learning

Deliberate study is efficient but should occupy no more than ~25% of total learning time.

**Word cards — the single most efficient vocabulary activity:**
- Foreign word on one side; translation on the other. Test yourself by covering one side.
- Start with 15–20 words per pack, increase to ~50.
- Space repetitions: few minutes → 1 hour → next day → 1 week → 2 weeks.
- Never include words from the same lexical set in the same pack.

**Pronunciation:** Prioritise early — remedial work later requires much more effort. Find out which sounds are genuinely difficult for speakers of your L1. Practice in isolation → common words → phrases → short sentences.

**Grammar:** Most grammar is learned incidentally through Strands 1, 2, and 4. Deliberate grammar study: target your known error patterns. Keep it under 25% of learning time.

## Strand 4 — Fluency Development

Fluency = speed and automaticity. Built by working with material you already know, with some pressure to go faster.

**Four requirements of any fluency activity:** Easy, familiar material · Some pressure to go faster · Large quantity of practice · Focus on meaning, not form.

## The Conditions That Make Learning Stick

These conditions underlie all the activities above. Any method that reliably creates them will work.

- **Spaced repetition** — Distribute repetitions over increasing time gaps. Massed review is far less effective than spaced sessions.
- **Retrieval practice** — Actively recall an item rather than passively re-reading it. Word cards force retrieval.
- **Varied meetings** — Meet the same word in different forms, contexts, and senses.
- **Varied use** — Using a word in speaking and writing has stronger effects than just meeting it in input.
- **Deliberate attention** — Noticing something intentionally dramatically increases the likelihood of retention.
- **Avoid interference** — Keep similar items (synonyms, opposites, lexical sets) separated in time and study packs.

<Callout>
Minimum repetitions for learning: at least 8, but more is always better. There is no magic number — only consistent, spaced, varied contact with items.
</Callout>

## Motivation and Time

**Self-Determination Theory:** Autonomous motivation — acting from internal reasons — predicts long-term success better than any technique. Three needs that must be met:
- **Autonomy** — choosing your materials and pace
- **Competence** — seeing clear skill growth
- **Relatedness** — genuine connection to the target language community

## Quick-Start Action Plan

**Week 1–2:** Download Nation's survival vocabulary and put all items on word cards. Drill with spaced repetition.

**Week 1 onwards:** Find a film in the target language; download its script. Study the script first, watch with subtitles, re-watch weeks later.

**Week 2 onwards:** Get a children's reader or Level 1 graded reader. Read carefully once (with dictionary), then twice quickly. Put repeated unknowns on word cards.

**Week 3 onwards:** Arrange a regular speaking session with a native speaker. Practice survival role plays multiple times per session.

**Daily:** 10–20 minutes of word card review. Do something in the language, even 5 minutes.

**Most common mistakes to avoid:**
- Spending more than 25% of time on deliberate study (grammar books, exercises)
- Learning words from lexical sets together (months, colours, opposites)
- Ignoring pronunciation in early stages — very hard to fix later
- Skipping fluency activities — knowing something ≠ using it quickly
- Reading material that is too difficult (more than 2 unknown words per 100)
