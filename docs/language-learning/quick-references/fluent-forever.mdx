Complete method · All 5 memory principles · Flash card systems · Tools & resources

## Chapter 2: Five Principles to End Forgetting

Core insight: Memories are networks of neural connections. Stronger networks = better recall. The SRS exploits these principles automatically.

**Principle 1 — Make Memories More Memorable**
Deeper processing = stronger memory (Craik & Lockhart, 1972). Four levels, shallowest to deepest:

<DataTable
  headers={["Level", "Example"]}
  rows={[
    ["Structure", "How many capital letters in BEAR? (shallowest — avoid this)"],
    ["Sound", "Does APPLE rhyme with Snapple? (activates ears/mouth)"],
    ["Concept", "Is TOOL another word for 'instrument'? (better still)"],
    ["Personal Connection", "Do you like PIZZA? (deepest — activates most brain regions)"],
  ]}
/>

Build connections at **all four levels** for every new word.

**Principle 2 — Maximise Laziness**
Only study what needs studying. Trust the SRS schedule. Adding new cards almost always beats over-reviewing old ones.

**Principle 3 — Don't Review, Recall**

<Callout label="The Testing Effect">
Testing yourself is 5× more effective than re-reading. Struggling to recall — even failing — strengthens the memory more than passive review. 5 min of self-testing ≈ 25 min of studying.
</Callout>

**Principle 4 — Wait, Wait! Don't Tell Me!**
Review a card right before you'd forget it. Anki calculates this automatically. Best review gap ≈ 10–20% of the total delay to the final test.

**Principle 5 — Rewrite the Past**
Each successful recall strengthens and updates the memory. Early memories keep improving with every retrieval — unlike static notes.

## Chapter 3: Pronunciation First

The 'broken word' problem: learn a word from reading without knowing its pronunciation and you create two separate words in your brain — the written form and the spoken form. These never properly connect.

<DataTable
  headers={["Training Target", "Method & Tools"]}
  rows={[
    ["Ear Training (hearing)", "Minimal pair tests: listen to near-identical sounds until you reliably distinguish them. Tools: Forvo.com, Fluent-Forever.com/chapter3 trainers"],
    ["Mouth Training (speaking)", "Learn tongue/lip position from IPA Decoder. Use back-chaining for hard words: say last syllable, then add one syllable at a time backward. Tools: italki.com tutors"],
    ["Eye Training (spelling)", "Flash cards: spelling → sound, sound → spelling. Your SRS drills the rules until they're automatic."],
  ]}
/>

**Building pronunciation flash cards (1–3 hours):** For each difficult sound, note the spelling, find an example word, get a Forvo.com recording, and make two cards: spelling → sound, sound → spelling. Learn 30 cards/day for 3–8 days.

## Chapter 4: First 625 Words

'Words are symphonies, not translations.' Every word contains sound, spelling, grammar, meaning, and personal connections. Translation strips all of this away.

<Callout label="Why 625 Words?">
These are the most common, concrete, easily visualisable words in any language. Learn them without any English — use pictures only. At 1,000 words you recognise ~75% of written text.
</Callout>

## Chapter 5: Grammar — Learned, Not Memorised

Key finding: Adults and children follow the same developmental stages when acquiring grammar, regardless of native language or method. The language acquisition machine never stops.

**What makes input comprehensible:** You understand the gist of the sentence, each sentence has only 1–2 new elements, it comes from a vetted source (grammar book, graded reader).

**Three flash card types for grammar:**

<DataTable
  headers={["Card Type", "What It Teaches"]}
  rows={[
    ["New Word Cards", "Fill-in-the-blank: 'He lives ___ New York City' → 'in'. Meaning + pronunciation in context."],
    ["Word Form Cards", "Fill-in-the-blank with hint: 'He ___ in New York City (to live)' → 'lives'. Conjugation/declension."],
    ["Word Order Cards", "Where does 'he' go in 'lives in New York City'? → 'He lives in New York City.' Sentence structure."],
  ]}

## Chapter 6: Fluency

Once you have pronunciation + 625 words + basic grammar, your language 'opens up.'

<DataTable
  headers={["Goal", "Vocabulary Target"]}
  rows={[
    ["Casual conversation", "Top 1,000 words + grammar = sufficient for most situations"],
    ["Reading fiction", "Top 2,000 words → ~90% comprehension in fiction"],
    ["Academic reading", "Top 2,000 + 570 academic words → ~90% academic comprehension"],
    ["Professional fluency", "Top 5,000 words + domain-specific vocabulary"],
    ["Near-native fluency", "12,500+ words across all domains"],
  ]}
/>

**Listening progression:** Children's TV (beginner) → TV shows you've seen in English (intermediate) → native TV/films without subtitles (advanced).

**Speaking:** italki.com tutors, language exchanges, Taboo-style circumlocution practice (describe things without using the obvious word).

## Quick Reference: Complete Method Checklist

**Phase 1 — Setup (Day 1):** Install Anki · Watch Anki tutorials · Get a grammar book · Get a pocket phrasebook

**Phase 2 — Pronunciation (Days 2–10):** Build pronunciation deck from IPA + Forvo recordings · Learn 30 cards/day for 3–8 days

**Phase 3 — First 625 Words (Weeks 2–12):** Search Google Images in target language · Create 2–3 flash cards per word · 20–30 words/day for 1–3 months

**Phase 4 — Grammar + Sentences (Months 2–6):** Work through grammar book · Create sentence flash cards · Submit writing to Lang-8.com weekly · Turn corrections into new flash cards

**Phase 5 — Fluency (Months 3+):** Audiobook + text reading · Children's TV → native content · italki tutors for speaking · 1 page/week on Lang-8

**Daily study habit:** 15–20 min SRS review + 10–15 min new cards = 30 min minimum. Optional: 15–60 min reading/listening.
