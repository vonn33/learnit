# Part II · Head to Head

This is where the real value of reading both books appears. Wyner gives you a complete system; Nation gives you principles to evaluate and extend any system. Together, they are more coherent than either alone.

## II-B · Where They Diverge

These are differences in emphasis, strategy, or philosophical stance — not necessarily disagreements. Understanding the difference matters.

### 1. Translation: Enemy vs. Tool

<Compare
  left={{
    title: "Wyner — Avoid translation",
    body: "Translation creates a dependency. The English bridge slows real-time fluency. Direct image → word encoding is the goal from day one."
  }}
  right={{
    title: "Nation — Use translation strategically",
    body: "Word cards use translation (foreign word on one side, L1 meaning on other). It is efficient for initial retention. What matters is eventual direct access, not the route taken."
  }}
/>

**Resolution:** Wyner's concern is valid long-term: translation-mediated knowledge adds a processing step that slows automaticity. Nation's pragmatism is valid short-term: translation is efficient for building initial form-meaning links. The research suggests the combination — image + translation + sound — often outperforms either alone for initial learning. The critical transition is actively weaning off the translation crutch through fluency practice, which is exactly what Nation's fluency strand (and Wyner's sentence mining) is designed to do.

### 2. Grammar: Implicit Exposure vs. Deliberate Balance

<Compare
  left={{
    title: "Wyner — Acquire grammar through exposure",
    body: "Grammar rules are largely unnecessary. The brain detects patterns from comprehensible input. Sentence mining + writing correction + native input is sufficient."
  }}
  right={{
    title: "Nation — Balance exposure with deliberate study",
    body: "Language study is one full strand (25%). Deliberate grammar attention accelerates acquisition of specific forms. Explicit and implicit learning are complementary, not competing."
  }}
/>

**Resolution:** The meta-analytic evidence (Norris & Ortega, 2000) clearly shows explicit grammar instruction accelerates acquisition of specific features beyond input alone, especially for adult learners. Wyner's grammar approach works, but Nation's prescription of deliberate grammar attention is better supported by the research. Wyner's noticing mechanism (correction workflow) is a partial implementation of the same principle — he gets there, but less systematically.

### 3. Output: Incidental vs. Dedicated Strand

<Compare
  left={{
    title: "Wyner — Output emerges from input",
    body: "Writing correction (Lang-8) is mentioned. Speaking is addressed but not as a systematic strand. The focus is heavily on building the knowledge base through input and flash cards."
  }}
  right={{
    title: "Nation — Output requires its own dedicated time",
    body: "Strand 2 (meaning-focused output) is 25% of total time. Production forces syntactic commitment that comprehension avoids. Role plays, prepared talks, free writing are all prescribed."
  }}
/>

**Resolution:** Nation is right here. The immersion research is definitive: learners with years of rich comprehensible input can still have persistent grammatical inaccuracies in production that more input does not fix (Swain, 1985). Speaking and writing must be practised, not waited for. Wyner's system needs this strand added explicitly.

### 4. Fluency: Absent vs. Central

<Compare
  left={{
    title: "Wyner — Fluency as emergent outcome",
    body: "Fluency is an eventual result of SRS mastery + enough input. No dedicated fluency strand or time-pressure activities are prescribed."
  }}
  right={{
    title: "Nation — Fluency requires explicit practice",
    body: "Strand 4 is specifically for automaticity with known material under time pressure. 4/3/2 speaking task, speed reading, repeated listening. Skill Acquisition Theory shows automaticity only comes through deliberate speed practice."
  }}
/>

**Resolution:** This is Wyner's clearest structural gap. Knowing vocabulary and grammar does not produce fluency automatically — Skill Acquisition Theory (Anderson, 1983; DeKeyser, 2001) is unambiguous: automaticity requires dedicated practice under speed pressure. Nation's fluency strand fills a hole in Wyner's system directly.

## II-C · Where They Genuinely Contradict

These are areas where reading one book and then the other produces an actual conflict, not just a difference of emphasis.

<Callout label="Genuine Tension 1 — The Role of Conscious Rule Learning">

**Wyner** (following Krashen): Conscious grammar rules play almost no role in acquisition. The brain builds its own rules implicitly from exposure.

**Nation** (following DeKeyser, Schmidt): Conscious noticing of grammatical form is necessary or at minimum strongly accelerating. Explicit instruction on specific forms works.

**Research verdict:** Nation is better supported. Norris & Ortega's (2000) meta-analysis showed explicit instruction produces significantly better outcomes than implicit exposure alone for specific forms. The acquisition-learning distinction (Krashen) is now considered oversimplified by most SLA researchers.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Genuine Tension 2 — Word Cards With Translation vs. Without">

**Wyner:** No English on cards. Ever. Images, audio, personal connections only. Translation builds dependency.

**Nation:** L1 translation on the back of word cards is efficient and effective for initial form-meaning mapping. Translation is a tool, not an enemy.

**Research verdict:** Split. For initial retention, translation is often comparable to or faster than image-only. For long-term fluency and automaticity, direct concept encoding wins. **Practical synthesis:** use translation as a scaffold for abstract or difficult words, but combine with image + sound where possible, and actively practice bypassing the translation cue during fluency activities.
</Callout>
