# Part III · The Synthesised View

When you strip away the method-specific details and look at what both books consistently agree on — and what the research behind both supports — eight master principles emerge. These survive any trend, tool, or technique preference.

---

## III-A · The Eight Master Principles

<Callout label="Principle 1 — Conditions beat methods">
Any method that reliably creates comprehensible input, spaced retrieval, output pressure, and noticing will work. Any method that neglects these will underperform, regardless of how compelling it sounds.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 2 — Spaced retrieval is the engine">
The single most evidence-backed intervention in language learning. Non-negotiable. Everything else is an optimisation on top of this.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 3 — Phonological precision is the foundation">
Sound → symbol mapping must be established early. Poor phonological representations degrade vocabulary retention and slow automaticity. Never skip this stage.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 4 — Frequency determines priority">
Learn the most frequent words first. The coverage return per word is disproportionately high in the high-frequency zone. The top 2,000 words cover ~90% of general text.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 5 — Output is not optional">
Production forces syntactic processing that comprehension bypasses. Speaking and writing must be practised, not waited for. Immersion research is definitive on this.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 6 — Fluency is a distinct skill">
Knowing a word and using it automatically are different states. Speed and automaticity require dedicated practice under time pressure — they do not emerge naturally from study.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 7 — Encode deeply, encounter variedly">
Multi-sensory encoding (sound + image + personal context) and varied encounter contexts (different sentences, topics, collocations) build robust, generalisable word knowledge.
</Callout>

<Callout label="Principle 8 — Autonomous motivation is the long game">
Techniques produce short-term gains. Intrinsic motivation — genuine interest, self-direction, meaningful connection to the language — determines who reaches fluency.
</Callout>

## III-B · Integrated Learning Framework

What a learner who has absorbed both books would actually do. This is the combined system — Wyner's concrete tools implemented inside Nation's balanced strand architecture.

### Phase 1 — Foundation (Weeks 1–4)

**Priority: Sound system before anything else (Wyner + Nation both agree)**

1. Set up Anki. Learn 10–20 new cards/day rhythm. Build the habit before the content.
2. Build pronunciation deck (1–3 hours): IPA + Forvo recordings + minimal pair tests. Learn over 3–8 days.
3. Memorise survival vocabulary (~120 items). Nation's free survival list. Covers immediate basic needs.
4. Start a grammar/pronunciation reference book. Read the introduction; understand the sound system structure. Do not study all grammar rules yet — use it as a reference only.

### Phase 2 — Core Vocabulary (Months 1–3)

**Priority: High-frequency words + first grammar exposure**

- **Strand 1 (Input, ~25%):** Graded readers at your level (max 1 unknown per 50 words). Read-while-listening where audio is available. Children's content in target language.
- **Strand 2 (Output, ~25%):** Memorised survival phrases in real situations. Basic prepared talk (2–3 sentences about yourself). Start a language exchange or find a tutor for low-pressure conversation.
- **Strand 3 (Study, ~25%):** Word cards for top 625 words — image + sound + personal connection (Wyner's card format). No near-synonyms in the same pack. Keyword technique for hard words. Note: start with translation on back for speed, then phase it out.
- **Strand 4 (Fluency, ~25%):** Repeated listening to known short material. Re-read graded texts. Repeat the same role-play scenario 3 times in a row (shrinking to shorter times = 4/3/2 precursor).

### Phase 3 — Grammar + Sentences (Months 3–6)

**Priority: Comprehensible input ramp-up + output structure**

- Add sentence cards to SRS deck. One unknown item per sentence. Mine from graded readers, shows you've watched, or textbook examples.
- Both comprehension cards (blank → word) and production cards (L1 sentence → target language version).
- Writing correction workflow: write short texts, get corrected (italki, Tandem, native speaker exchange). Compare your version to the correction — this is noticing in practice.
- Deliberate grammar focus (15–20 min sessions): target your known error patterns. Substitution tables. One structure at a time.
- 4/3/2 speaking task: give a prepared talk to a partner in 4 min, then 3 min, then 2 min. Nation's most actionable fluency activity.

### Phase 4 — Advanced and Maintenance (Month 6+)

**Priority: Authentic input + natural fluency**

- Graduate to authentic content (real films, podcasts, books) as vocabulary allows.
- Monitor coverage: if more than ~2 unknowns per 100 words, material is too hard — switch to slightly easier material or narrow reading.
- Check your vocabulary size regularly (vocabularysize.com or equivalents). Set clear milestones: 2,000 words → comfortable graded reading; 5,000 → comfortable authentic reading; 8,000 → newspapers/novels.
- Continue Anki daily. As the deck matures, daily review time naturally drops. The system becomes low-maintenance at this stage.
- Protect enjoyment: follow genuine interests in the target language. This is the SDT autonomy condition — critical for surviving the plateau.
