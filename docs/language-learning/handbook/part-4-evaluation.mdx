# Part IV · Critical Evaluation

These are weaknesses neither book adequately addresses, and key findings post-2014 that update the books' positions. They are not criticisms of the authors — both books are products of their time — but they matter for a complete picture.

---

## IV-A · Shared Blind Spots

### 1 — Individual Differences

Both books present universal prescriptions. Nation's 25-25-25-25 and Wyner's sequence work well on average, but individual variation in phonological memory capacity, language aptitude, working memory, prior linguistic experience, and L1 distance from the target language significantly affects what the optimal balance actually is for any given learner.

**Practical adjustment:** Audit your own bottleneck. If you have broad vocabulary but poor automaticity — over-invest in Strand 4. If pronunciation is your persistent weakness — over-invest in ear training before vocabulary. The frameworks are starting points, not rigid schedules.

### 2 — Anxiety and Affective Factors

Krashen's Affective Filter concept is mentioned in Wyner but not deeply addressed in either book. High anxiety measurably raises cortisol levels, which reduces hippocampal neuroplasticity — the biological substrate of new memory formation. High-pressure output activities can raise anxiety enough to inhibit acquisition even while improving performance metrics.

**Practical adjustment:** Protect low-stakes practice environments, especially early. Nation's prepared talks (you write the content, you choose the topic) are better than cold spontaneous speaking for anxious learners. Wyner's self-paced SRS is naturally low-anxiety. Build confidence before intensity.

### 3 — Sleep and Consolidation Timing

Neither book addresses when to study relative to sleep, which post-2014 research has shown is material. During slow-wave sleep and REM, the hippocampus replays recently encoded memories and transfers them to the neocortex for long-term storage. Sleep immediately after learning produces dramatically better next-day retention.

**Practical adjustment:** Schedule new card learning and new sentence reading in the evening before sleep. Morning sessions are best for retrieval practice (reviewing existing material), not for first encoding of new items.

### 4 — Pragmatic and Sociocultural Competence

Both books focus on linguistic competence: phonology, vocabulary, grammar, fluency. Neither fully addresses pragmatic competence — knowing how to use language appropriately across different social contexts, registers, and cultural norms. A learner can be grammatically accurate but consistently socially inappropriate.

**Practical adjustment:** Supplement both books with deliberate exposure to authentic social interaction — podcasts, interviews, soap operas, and conversation partners expose pragmatic patterns that textbooks miss.

### 5 — Technology Integration

Both books predate AI conversation partners, AI pronunciation coaches, neural machine translation, and adaptive deep-learning SRS systems.

**Post-2014 tools worth integrating:**
- **AI conversation partners (ChatGPT, Claude, language apps):** Low-anxiety speaking/writing practice available 24/7. Supplement for volume and confidence building, not a replacement for human interaction.
- **AI pronunciation feedback (ELSA, Speechling, Pimsleur):** Automated phonological feedback at a level of detail unavailable without a specialist teacher.
- **Adaptive SRS (Anki, RemNote):** 2025 research shows deep-learning adaptive algorithms substantially outperform fixed SM-2 scheduling for individual forgetting curve modelling.
- **Corpus tools (SketchEngine, Netspeak, Wordandphrase.info):** Show real-world frequency and collocation patterns. Fills the 'use' dimension of word knowledge that flashcards miss.

<Callout label="The Overarching Evidence">
Language learning is not mysterious. It is a predictable outcome of reliably creating the right cognitive conditions over sufficient time. The spacing effect, retrieval practice, levels of processing, phonological encoding, output pressure, and automatisation practice each have decades of evidence behind them. The two books together operationalise all of them.
</Callout>
